Philo the Logician &
Barb the Syllogizer
Teach Logic on the Web

Professor Wengert at the University of Illinois uses Java Applets and Prolog to teach formal logic in his Philosophy courses. He has two applications: Philo the Logician and Barb the Syllogizer.

Java Applets provide the user interface for his students.This allows them to use formal logic symbols when entering their answers to the exercises.The applets communicate with Prolog on the Web server (with Java acting as an intermediary). The parsing and analysis is all done in Amzi! Prolog and the results are returned to the applet.

Philo the Logician

Philo the Logician has a number of examples. When you select one, the exemplar sentence will appear in the upper window along with an explanation of what the variables p, q, etc. are to stand for. The student types their formula in the top box, using the buttons to enter symbols, then clicks the Parse button. If the formula is well-formed, this will write your formula in "Canonical" form (with all the parentheses and with the scoping order of each connective). Pressing the Submit displays what the formula would say in English in the lower window, and will then tell you whether or not your formula is correct. If not, it will give the student a case where their formula differs from the correct answer, and explain whether in that case their formula would be true or false.

Barb the Syllogizer

Barb the Syllogizer gives some premises and invites the student to type in a conclusion, in English, that follows. The application tells the student whether their conclusion follows, along with an attempt to explain why it does or does not follow.

Students may choose to use traditional or modern rules. On the traditional rules it is assumed that the subject of a universal statement holds of at least one thing, while on modern rules there is no such assumption. Thus on traditional rules from "All attorneys are wealthy," one could conclude that there are attorneys and that they are wealthy. Modern logic understands the statement as saying "If there are any attorneys then they are wealthy," which could be true because there are no attorneys at all. Some of the examples have more than two premises, this is not purely syllogistic, but is a general monadic predicate calculus prover.
